She had done this four hundred and thirty-one times.

The ship knew the exact number, because the ship counted everything. Transit durations, energy expenditure, the particular way the drift engine folded into readiness in the nanoseconds before submersion. It monitored her heart rate and her breathing and the electrical weather of her brain, and it filed all of this into neat columns that she never read. She had looked at the data once, years ago, after her twelfth or fifteenth run. Her heart slowed when she entered the foam. Her breathing became so shallow it barely registered. The electrical activity in her cortex did something the monitoring system classified as anomalous and could not further describe. She had closed the file and never opened it again. Some things did not benefit from measurement.

The cockpit was small and unadorned. A seat, a console, a curved viewport showing nothing worth seeing at this distance from anything. She ran the pre-drift sequence the way she always did, the way she had done it hundreds of times before: checking the engine alignment, confirming coordinates, ensuring the ship's autonomous systems could handle the return if necessary. A contingency. Standard procedure. She did not dwell on why the contingency existed.

There had been a time when crossing the stars meant force. The cascade drives, those early brutal engines, had folded spacetime like a fist crushing paper, punching ships through the crumpled geometry by sheer mechanical will. They had been marvels once. They had opened the near systems and then the far ones, had carried colony transports and warships and the full machinery of empire to every star within reach. And they had carried war. The great wars, plural, because there was never just one, fought first between neighbouring systems and then across the spiral arms, fleet against fleet in the long burning centuries when humanity discovered that the universe was neither empty nor entirely welcoming and responded the only way a frightened species knows how. Cascade drives had built civilisations and then ended them. Now they sat in museums and history modules, artefacts of an age when starflight meant wrestling the universe into submission, and children looked at them the way children once looked at steam engines: with curiosity, and a faint incomprehension that anyone had ever lived that way.

The drift was different. The drift did not fight.

She pressed the final confirmation and the engine woke beneath her, not with the violent shudder of the old drives but with something closer to a breath. A hum that she felt less in her ears than in the architecture of her thoughts, as though some frequency just below perception had begun to resonate with the space between her neurons. The viewport display went dark. The console lights dimmed. And then the ship, the seat, the small utilitarian cockpit with its hard edges and mechanical certainties, all of it fell away, and she was nowhere and everywhere at once.

The transition was like sinking into deep water.

She had tried to explain it once, to someone who had never drifted, and had failed in the way everyone failed. Language was built for a world of surfaces and edges, of here and there, of I and not-I. The drift dissolved all of those categories. The closest she had come was this: imagine you are standing at the edge of an ocean, and the ocean is not water but the fabric of reality itself, the quantum foam that seethes beneath the smooth skin of the observable universe, a turbulence of spacetime so fundamental that matter and energy and distance and time are just patterns on its surface, waves that rise and fall and are never the same twice. Now imagine stepping in. Not with your body, but with your mind, your awareness, everything that you are. The water rises past your ankles, your waist, your chest. There is a moment of pressure, of resistance, the animal part of the brain that knows it cannot breathe here, that what waits below the surface is not meant for human consciousness. And then, like a free diver passing through the thermocline into the silent deep, the resistance breaks, and you stop fighting, and you let go.

And you are the ocean.

She spread across the foam like ink in water.

Not she. Not anymore. The pronouns stopped making sense in the drift. There was no she, no pilot, no woman in a cockpit above a small and unremarkable world. There was only awareness, vast and unbounded, draped across the quantum substrate of the universe like cloth settling over the shape of everything that existed. She could feel it all. Not see, not hear, not touch, but something older and more fundamental than any sense the body had ever offered. The foam had texture, topology, a landscape of peaks and troughs that corresponded to every concentration of mass and energy in the cosmos. Stars were bright warm swells, gentle hills of curved spacetime that she rolled over like tides. Black holes were depths, abyssal trenches in the foam so steep and so smooth that awareness slid towards them and had to be gently, instinctively redirected, the way a swimmer adjusts without thinking to a current. Planets were smaller undulations. Moons were ripples. And life, life was a particular kind of warmth. A frequency. Wherever living things gathered in sufficient density she could feel them the way a hand held above a candle feels heat: not the individual flames, but the collective radiance. A billion small fires burning on the surface of a world, each one a mind, each mind a tiny peak in the foam, and all of them together a gentle, shimmering glow.

She had felt things in the drift that she had no names for. Structures so vast they could only be ships, but ships of a scale and age that defied comprehension, drifting in the spaces between dead stars like cathedrals adrift in the dark. She did not know what they were. She did not think anyone did. They were old. They were impossibly, achingly old, and when her awareness passed near them she felt something that might have been recognition, or might have been the ghost of it, an echo of a human origin so ancient it had fossilised into something else entirely. She never lingered near them. Not because she was afraid, but because the sadness that radiated from those vast and patient structures was so deep and so still that she worried it might follow her home.

But the rest of it. The rest of it was something for which no word had been built.

Every drifter she had ever spoken to said the same thing, and then fell silent, because the same thing was always inadequate. Euphoria was too small. Ecstasy was too brief. It was more like wholeness. For the duration of the drift, the boundaries that defined a human life, the walls of skull and skin, the cruelty of only ever being one thing in one place at one point in time, all of it dissolved, and what remained was a consciousness so expanded that the entire universe fit inside it, or it fit inside the universe, and the distinction no longer held. She could reach out and brush the surface of a star ten thousand light-years distant and feel its particular warmth, its age, the way it sang in frequencies that only the foam could carry. She could trace the filaments of dark matter that threaded between the stars and feel the architecture of the galaxy beneath her awareness like the grain of wood beneath a hand. She could sink into the deep places, the great voids between the spiral arms where the foam was thin and almost silent, and float there in a quiet so profound it felt sacred.

But not hold it. Not all of it. Not quite.

The engine had no limits that anyone had found. The mathematics did not care about distance. A point across the room and a point across the universe were equally valid solutions, equally reachable, equally real. But the mind inside the engine was still a human mind, still an animal thing built from carbon and electricity and a few billion years of evolution on a single unremarkable world, and it could not hold the whole of creation in focus any more than an eye could read every page of every book in a library at once. Within the galaxy, the foam was vivid, sharp, navigable. She could feel the precise topology of a world on the far side of the spiral arms and collapse to it with the confidence of a hand reaching for a familiar door handle. It might take hours. It might take days. Time had no texture in the drift, no weight, no passage that the mind could feel, but the body in the cockpit still breathed, still aged, still ticked along in the patient animal rhythm of a heart that did not know it was supposed to be everywhere at once. A crossing of the galaxy was not instantaneous. It was a journey, even if the traveller could not feel herself travelling.

And beyond the galaxy, the focus failed.

She could feel them out there. Other galaxies, other vast spiralling densities of matter and warmth and life, hanging in the foam like lanterns seen through fog. She could feel their weight, their collective hum, the particular texture of their presence in the quantum substrate. But she could not resolve them. The further her awareness reached, the more the peaks and troughs of the foam seemed to smear and blur, like text at the edge of vision, like trying to hold the details of a dream in the seconds after waking. She could feel that something was there. She could not feel it clearly enough to arrive. The other galaxies were ghosts, shimmering at the far edge of what a human consciousness could grasp, close enough to ache for and too far to reach.

Sometimes, at the very limit of that blurred perception, she thought she felt something looking back. Not the passive warmth of living things, not the familiar signatures of matter and energy, but something that felt like awareness itself, vast and focused and turned in her direction. She could never hold it long enough to be sure. It was always at the edge, always dissolving when she reached for it, and afterwards she could never say with certainty whether she had felt another mind in the foam or simply the echo of her own, reflected back from a distance too great for her to comprehend.

She did not talk about this. None of the drifters did.

This was why the drift had ended the wars. Not through treaty or exhaustion or the weary arithmetic of attrition, but through revelation. When you had been the universe, when you had felt every living thing as a point of warmth in the foam, the idea of destroying any of it became incoherent. Obscene. Like burning a part of your own body. The wars had simply stopped, not all at once but in a slow tide of refusal, as more and more of those who had drifted found they could no longer raise weapons against what they had touched. The generals and admirals and politicians who had never entered the foam could not understand it. They called it weakness, then treason, then a kind of madness. But the drifters knew what the others did not. They had been everywhere. They had felt everything. And after that, the old arguments about territory and resources and the lines between us and them sounded like arguments about which drop of water owned the ocean.

No one could challenge humanity now, not in this galaxy. Not because of weapons or fleets or the machinery of force, but because a species that could move through the quantum foam of spacetime itself, that could cross from one arm of the spiral to the other in the time it took a body to sleep and wake, had simply become something that conflict could not touch. What was there to fight over? What border could you draw in the foam? And if something existed in those other galaxies, something whose awareness did not blur at the distances that defeated human minds, something that could hold the whole of the foam in focus the way a drifter held a single galaxy, then it had not yet made itself known in any way that could be understood. Perhaps it had no reason to. Perhaps it was already here, watching from the smeared edge of perception, patient and incomprehensible, waiting for humanity to learn to see more clearly. Or perhaps the ghosts at the edge of the foam were nothing at all, and humanity drifted alone through a universe that was vast and warm and empty of anything that matched them.

The destination was ahead of her now. Or rather, it was a point in the foam that her awareness had been drifting towards all along, a particular density, a specific set of coordinates that the ship's engine held in some deep mechanical layer far beneath her consciousness. She felt it the way you feel a word you are about to speak: not yet formed, but already shaping itself, already inevitable. A world. A port. A place with a name she would remember when she had a mouth to say it with and lungs to push the air through.

It was time to come back.

The collapse was the hardest part. Not physically, not mechanically. The engine handled all of that, translating the mathematics of quantum probability back into the crude fact of a ship arriving at a point in space. The hard part was the choice. The decision to stop being everything and become one thing again. One body. One location. One heartbeat in one chest in one small cockpit above one unremarkable world out of hundreds of billions. Every drifter knew this moment. The moment where you hung suspended in the foam, spread across light-years, feeling the warmth of a trillion living things and the ancient slow grief of those vast structures drifting between dead stars, and you had to choose to let it all go.

She had always come back. Four hundred and thirty-one times she had felt the destination resolve and made the choice and collapsed back into her body and opened her eyes in the small cockpit and felt the terrible, suffocating smallness of being a single point in space. Four hundred and thirty-one times she had sat in the silence afterwards and breathed and waited for the loss to become bearable and told herself that this was fine. That she did this because she was good at it. That she enjoyed her work. That the next run was already scheduled, already waiting, and she could go back whenever she wanted, and the fact that she always wanted to was not something that required examination.

The destination pulsed gently in the foam, blue-green and bright with the warmth of eight billion small fires.

Her coordinates. Her mandate. The ship beneath her, so far beneath her, so small and solid and impossibly distant, was ready for the return.

She reached for the collapse.

And paused.

Just another moment. Just one more breath of the infinite, one more heartbeat of being everywhere and nowhere and everything all at once. The foam held her, warm and vast and patient, and the stars rolled beneath her like hills in a country she had known all her life, and somewhere, impossibly far away and impossibly close, a cathedral drifted in the dark, ancient beyond all comprehension, carrying the faintest ghost of a wish she almost recognised.

Just another moment.
