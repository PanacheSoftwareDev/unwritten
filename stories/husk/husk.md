description: "In a future where corporations send workers to the stars by tearing them apart and rebuilding them light-years away, one woman returns home to find out what it costs to come back."
---

The first thing Mara noticed was the taste. Copper and saline, like she'd bitten the inside of her cheek, except she hadn't. This mouth had never bitten anything. It was three minutes old.

It had been the same the first time — waking up on the Kepler station eighteen months ago, that same metallic flood across a tongue that hadn't existed moments before. She'd panicked then. Thrashed on the table, gasped for air that her new lungs didn't yet know how to process, and a technician had pinned her shoulders down until her nervous system caught up with itself. This time she knew what was coming. That didn't make it better. Familiarity just meant she could catalogue the wrongness more precisely.

She was lying on something cold and slightly concave, naked, and there was a woman in blue scrubs peeling sensor pads from her chest. The light above was the flat, indifferent white of a corporation that had requisitioned the sun and made it boring. She tried to sit up and her arms didn't work properly. Not yet. The signals were all there but the muscle memory was still compiling, her brain running diagnostics on a body it recognised as its own but had never actually used. Last time the calibration had taken longer — nearly five minutes before she could close her fist. This body was faster. She didn't know if that meant the printers were improving or if this version of her was slightly different from the last one. She tried not to think about which possibility was worse.

"Don't try to move for another minute," the woman in scrubs said without looking at her. She'd said this many times, to many people. "Your motor functions will calibrate. It's normal."

Mara already knew. She lay still and stared at the ceiling and tried to decide if she was alive.

That was the question, wasn't it. The one they'd walked her through in the medical briefing eighteen months ago, before the first transfer, in a beige conference room in the Drayton-Callister building in Houston. A woman with kind eyes and a legal pad had explained it carefully: the scanning process reads you at the quantum level, and the act of reading is the act of unmaking. You can't copy a quantum state without destroying it. That's not a limitation of the technology — it's physics. The you that steps into the scanner on Earth will cease to exist. The you that wakes up at the other end will have every memory, every neural pathway, every feeling you've ever had. They will *be* you in every measurable sense. But the original is gone.

Mara had nodded. She'd understood it the way you understand that the sun will eventually consume the Earth — as a fact too large to be frightening. She'd been sleeping in her truck for two weeks by then, parked behind a strip mall in Amarillo, rationing the last of the money she'd made selling the equipment from the farm. Cole and her father had kept the land, not that it would do them any good. The topsoil was gone. You could stand in the yard and watch it leave, carried east on a wind that never stopped, a slow brown migration of everything that had ever made the plains worth living on.

So she'd signed the contract. All nine hundred and forty-three pages of it.

That was something people didn't talk about — the contract. You heard about the ATLAS programme on the feeds, saw the Drayton-Callister ads with their sunrise imagery and the tagline *Build Tomorrow, Today*, and you imagined it was like signing up for a tough job in a remote location. An oil rig, maybe, or a deep-sea platform. But the contract told you the truth, if you had the patience to read it, which most people didn't.

Page sixteen was the one that stayed with her. She'd sat in the processing centre at four in the morning, the only applicant still reading while the others had scrolled to the end and signed, and on page sixteen she found the clause that required her to file a certificate of death. Her own. Pre-dated to the day of transfer. Below that, a clause assigning all her assets, possessions, and legal identity to Drayton-Callister Holdings, to be held in trust and re-assigned to her "reconstituted identity" upon completion of contract. There was a word for what that meant, buried in the legal language: the person signing the contract was agreeing to die. The person who would return was, legally, a new entity inheriting the old one's life.

She'd signed it anyway. There was nothing in Amarillo.

"You can sit up now."

Mara sat up. Her body worked. It felt like hers — the same slight ache in her left knee from when she'd fallen off the grain silo at fourteen, the same calluses on her palms, though these ones were fresh prints of calluses rather than earned ones. She reached up and touched the back of her neck, just below the hairline. There it was. The plug. A small disc flush with her skin, warm. The base node. The thing she'd been rebuilt from. But it was different this time. On the Kepler station the plug had been perfectly round, smooth as glass. This one had a slightly raised edge on one side, an almost imperceptible ridge, as though the printer had interpreted the blueprint fractionally differently. The same design, not quite the same execution. She pressed her fingertip against the ridge and felt a chill that had nothing to do with the temperature of the room.

In the medical briefing, before the first transfer, they'd installed the plug in a minor surgical procedure. It contained the quantum seed — the anchor point from which the bio-printers could reconstruct a complete human body. One plug at the origin, one at the destination. It stayed with you. It was, in a sense she tried not to think about too hard, more you than you were. The printers themselves had been sent ahead on unmanned probes, years before the generation ships launched — fast, light vessels carrying nothing but fabrication equipment and the ATLAS infrastructure. By the time the *Erebus* and the *Demeter* departed with their thousands of colonists preserved whole in stasis, the printers were already operational at the destination. And so the corporations had found a use for the gap: send workers ahead by teleportation to build the settlements, the ports, the habitats that the sleeping colonists would wake up to in a hundred years. The colonists would arrive to a world made ready for them, and they would never have been taken apart. They would step out of their stasis pods with the same bodies they'd gone to sleep in, continuous, unbroken, whole. The workers who'd built everything they'd inherit had been killed twice — once at each end.

The woman in scrubs handed her a grey cotton gown and gestured toward a door. "Processing is through there. Welcome home."

Mara didn't say thank you. She wasn't sure who would be saying it.

---

Processing took four hours. She sat in a series of identical rooms and signed a series of forms that felt like photocopies of the ones she'd signed eighteen months ago, only in reverse. A man with thinning hair and a Drayton-Callister lanyard explained that her assets were being transferred back to her reconstituted identity. Her apartment lease, which the corporation had maintained in her absence, was active. Her bank account, frozen on the day of her transfer, was now accessible. There was a modest completion bonus, already deposited. He said all of this the way someone reads a safety card on an aircraft — with the mechanical warmth of a script delivered a thousand times.

"Do you have any questions about reintegration support services?"

Mara shook her head.

"There's a number you can call. Counselling, job placement, that sort of thing. A lot of returnees find the adjustment period—"

"I'm fine."

He looked at her for a moment with something that might have been sympathy or might have been the look you give a piece of equipment you've finished servicing.

"You'll want to keep the area around the plug clean for the next week or so. It's sealed, but the skin around it is new. Any redness or discharge, go to a clinic." He paused. "A Drayton-Callister clinic. Your reintegration health coverage won't be accepted at most public facilities."

She took the information pack he offered — a thin folder with the sunrise logo — and walked out through the lobby. It was vast and clean, all glass and pale stone, with a holographic display that filled an entire wall. It cycled through images of distant planets — the rust-orange deserts of Kepler-442b, the ice fields of Ross 128 — and below them, a ticker: *EREBUS: 1,400 passengers, 38.2 light-years to destination, ETA 2197. DEMETER: 2,200 passengers, 11.4 light-years, ETA 2146. ATLAS infrastructure on schedule.* The faces of sleeping colonists in stasis pods, serene and blue-lit, drifted across the display like a screensaver for the end of the world. *You Built This*, the text read. *Welcome Back.*

Outside, it was Houston in August, and the heat hit her like a wall. But it was Earth heat — heavy and wet, full of the smell of hot concrete and exhaust and something faintly sweet from a food cart across the street. To the south, beyond the cluttered skyline, the Galveston Tether rose from the coast — a thread of carbon and steel so impossibly thin and tall that it looked like a scratch on the sky, vanishing upward into a point that wasn't quite visible, where the orbital platforms hung in geosynchronous silence. They'd been building it since before she was born. It was the largest single structure humanity had ever made, and from down here it looked like nothing at all.

She stood on the pavement and breathed it in. The air tasted different to her now, though she knew it couldn't. These lungs had never breathed before today, but they carried the memory of every breath she'd ever taken. The memory of Kansas wind, dry and carrying dust. The memory of the thin, recycled air of the Kepler-442 station, which always smelled faintly of ozone and plastic. Kepler-442b — a rocky world with a confirmed nitrogen-oxygen atmosphere orbiting a star a hundred light-years away. They'd found liquid water there in the forties. That had been enough. That single data point had redirected the entire economic machinery of the planet toward getting people to a place most of them would never see.

She flagged down a transit pod and gave it her address. She had an apartment. She had money in her account. She had her name back, legally speaking.

The pod hummed through streets that told two stories at once. A Drayton-Callister recruitment billboard — *YOUR SKILLS. NEW HORIZONS.* — towered over a block where half the storefronts were shuttered. The feed screen in the pod ran a news ticker: a water main had broken in the Third Ward for the second time this month; a congressional vote on offworld infrastructure funding had passed with bipartisan support; the *Demeter* had crossed a navigational waypoint and sent back telemetry confirming all passengers in stasis were viable. The city was crumbling and reaching for the stars simultaneously, and it didn't seem to notice the contradiction.

She touched the plug at the back of her neck and watched Houston slide past the window, and she tried to feel like she had come home rather than arrived somewhere for the first time.

---

The apartment was on the ninth floor of a building in Midtown that had probably been nice twenty years ago. Now it was the kind of place where the elevator worked on alternate days and the hallway lighting had a yellowish flicker that made everyone look slightly ill. But it was hers — or it had been hers, before, and now it was hers again, according to a transfer of tenancy document she'd signed in processing.

She stood in the doorway and looked at it. A studio, small. A bed against the far wall, a kitchenette with a two-burner stove, a window that faced another building. She'd lived here for three months before the transfer, long enough to unpack but not long enough to make it feel like anything. Her things were where she'd left them. A jacket hung on the back of the door. A mug on the counter, washed and placed upside down on a towel, which she must have done the morning she left. Drayton-Callister had maintained the lease, but nobody had been inside. It was a time capsule of a person who no longer existed.

She sat on the edge of the bed and waited for something to happen inside her. Some feeling of return, of arrival. It didn't come. She felt like a guest in someone else's room who happened to know where everything was.

That night she lay in the dark and listened to Houston through the window — the hum of traffic, someone's music two floors down, the distant chime of a transit pod. She pressed her fingers to the plug at the back of her neck. It was warm and smooth and she hated it. She hated that it was the most real thing about her.

---

The days acquired a routine without her choosing one. She woke up. She made coffee in the mug she'd left on the counter. She sat by the window and watched the street below, where people moved through their lives with the unselfconscious momentum of people who had been themselves continuously, without interruption, for their entire lives.

She called Cole on the third day. The connection was bad — it always was out in the plains, where the infrastructure was thirty years old and nobody was coming to replace it. The federal money went to launch facilities, orbital platforms, shipyards. The plains got nothing. Cole's face on the screen was tanned and thinner than she remembered, pixelated and stuttering on a network that had been left to rot. Behind him she could see the yard, the barn, the sky that went on forever in a way that Houston's sky couldn't, hemmed in as it was by glass and steel and the faint vertical line of the Tether in the distance.

"You're back," he said. Not a question.

"Got in a few days ago."

He nodded. There was a pause that lasted too long. She could see him looking at her — at her face, her eyes — the way you'd look at a photograph of someone you loved, searching for the thing that made it different from the real person.

"Dad wants to talk to you," he said. "He won't say it, but he does."

"How is he?"

"The same. The west field's gone. Soil's justite now." He paused again. "Are you okay?"

She almost laughed. It was such a normal question, and there was no normal answer. *I'm fine, Cole. I died eighteen months ago and now I'm someone who remembers being your sister.*

"I'm good," she said. "Just settling back in."

After they hung up she sat for a long time looking at the blank screen. He hadn't said it. He was too kind, and too careful, and probably didn't even fully understand it himself. But she'd heard it in the pause, in the way he'd looked at her. The slight distance. The almost imperceptible recalibration of someone who believed, on some level he might not even be able to articulate, that the person on the screen wasn't quite his sister.

---

On the fifth day she left the apartment to buy groceries. The store was two blocks away, a FreshCo franchise wedged between a vape shop and a pawn broker. She filled a basket — rice, tinned beans, coffee, eggs — and stood in the checkout line behind a woman with a toddler on her hip. Normal things. The scanner beeped. The toddler stared at her with the uncomplicated intensity of a child who hadn't yet learned what to think about anything.

On the way back she passed a bar. It was the kind of place that had a neon sign with two letters burned out and a chalkboard menu in the window advertising drink specials. Below the specials, in smaller letters, someone had written: **NO HUSKS.**

She stopped. She'd heard the word before, of course. Back in Kansas it had been an abstract thing — something you saw referenced on the feeds, a talking point for politicians and preachers. *These so-called people*, a senator had said on a broadcast she'd half-watched in a motel room somewhere in Oklahoma, *have no souls. They are husks. Shells. And we should not be forced to pretend otherwise.* She'd changed the channel. It hadn't been about her then.

She walked past the bar and didn't look back.

Over the following days she saw it more. A sticker on a lamppost — a crude drawing of a human outline, hollow inside, with a red line through it. A feed ad for a political campaign: *KEEP HOUSTON HUMAN. Vote Rawlings.* A conversation she overheard in the grocery store between two men in work clothes: "— buddy of mine hired one, didn't know, found the plug on the back of his neck in the locker room. Fired him that afternoon. Said it was like finding out you'd been working next to a corpse —"

She started wearing her hair down, making sure it covered the back of her neck.

---

She applied for jobs. Data entry, warehouse logistics, service work — anything that didn't require the kind of qualifications she'd never had the chance to get. She'd been good with machines on the farm, good with her hands, quick to learn. On the Kepler station she'd started on a fabrication crew, assembling habitat modules from printer-extruded components, and within six months they'd made her a team lead — youngest on the station, overseeing a shift of twelve. She'd managed supply manifests, resolved structural faults in pressurised environments, kept people alive in a place where a single mistake could kill a crew. None of it translated into a résumé that meant anything in Houston. The experience wasn't accredited. The qualifications weren't recognised. Eighteen months of offworld construction leadership reduced to a checkbox on an application form.

The applications all asked the same question, buried in the personal information section, between *Do you have a valid work permit?* and *Have you ever been convicted of a felony?*

**Have you participated in the ATLAS programme? Y/N**

She checked Y the first three times and heard nothing back. The fourth time she checked N and got a callback within two days. A warehouse in the Ship Channel district, sorting freight containers. She went to the interview and the foreman shook her hand and looked at her like a person and she felt something loosen in her chest for the first time since she'd woken up on the reconstruction table. He offered her a trial shift starting Monday.

On Friday she got an automated message. The offer had been rescinded. The background check had flagged her ATLAS participation. The message was one line: *We regret that we are unable to proceed with your application at this time.*

She sat on the edge of her bed and read it three times. Then she put the phone down and stared at the wall and felt the specific, quiet despair of a person who has done everything they were supposed to do and been punished for it anyway.

---

Weeks passed. The completion bonus from Drayton-Callister sat in her account, shrinking incrementally — rent, food, transit. She calculated that she had four months before it ran out. Maybe five if she stopped buying coffee.

She went for walks because the apartment made her feel like she was in a box. Houston in late summer was brutal, the air thick enough to chew, and she walked through it with her hair stuck to the back of her neck, always down, always covering the plug. She passed a public school with plywood over its windows, and across the street from it a gleaming Drayton-Callister recruitment centre with climate-controlled air spilling out every time the doors opened. She passed a city park where the grass had died and been replaced by grey synthetic turf that shimmered with heat. A billboard above it showed a green valley under an alien sun: *KEPLER-442b. THE NEXT CHAPTER.* Somewhere up there, a hundred light-years away, corporations were building the world that this one was being gutted to fund.

She walked past the Drayton-Callister regional headquarters once, the same gleaming tower she'd entered eighteen months ago as an applicant, and found a crowd outside. Thirty or forty people with signs. **HUSKS ARE NOT HUMAN. STOP PLAYING GOD. BRING BACK THE DEAD.** A few of them were shouting at the employees entering the building. A woman in a business suit walked through the protest line with her eyes straight ahead, the practised blankness of someone who'd learned to navigate this particular obstacle.

Mara watched from across the street. She understood the protesters and she hated them. She understood them because the thing they were afraid of was the thing she was afraid of too — that the process didn't just move you, it killed you and made something else. And she hated them because they'd never had to choose between that fear and sleeping in a truck in Amarillo.

She turned and walked home.

---

The days blurred. She stopped going out much. She cooked rice and beans and sat by the window and watched the street and tried not to think about the version of herself that had walked into the scanner in Houston eighteen months ago. Not the return — she'd already been a copy by then, a reconstruction stepping into a machine for the second time. She thought about the first time. The original. The version of her that had driven from Amarillo to the Drayton-Callister facility with everything she owned in the back of a truck, who had sat in a waiting room at four in the morning reading a nine hundred and forty-three page contract, who had walked into the scanner on legs that had carried her since birth and felt the hum of the machine building around her and known, in that final moment, what was about to happen. That woman — the real Mara, the only Mara who had ever been continuous from the day she was born — had been destroyed. Everything since then was an inheritance. A copy remembering a copy remembering the original, like a photograph of a photograph, each generation just slightly less sharp.

She wondered if this was what the protesters meant. Not that she wasn't human, but that there was a gap — a seam — where the continuity of experience had been broken, and no amount of perfect reconstruction could stitch it closed.

Some nights she dreamed about the farm. The old farmhouse, the sound of the screen door, her mother's voice calling from the kitchen. In the dreams she was herself, the original herself, small and whole and continuous. She woke from these dreams gasping, not with fear but with grief, the way you'd grieve for someone you'd lost, except the someone was her.

---

It was a Tuesday night — or maybe a Wednesday, the days had stopped mattering — when she heard it. A sound at the door. Not a knock. Something quieter. A hand on the handle, testing it. The faint scrape of metal on metal.

It was past midnight. The building was the kind of place where you heard your neighbours through the walls — arguments, music, the low murmur of screens — but nobody came to your door at midnight. Not in this part of town. Not for anything good.

She got up from the bed, slowly, and stood in the dark of the studio listening. The sound came again. Not forceful. Tentative, almost. Like someone who wasn't sure they wanted to come in.

She crossed the room in four steps and put her hand on the door. There was no peephole — the building was too old, too cheap. She pressed her ear to the wood and heard breathing. Close. Right on the other side.

"Who's there?"

Silence. Then, very quietly, a sound that might have been a word or might have been a breath catching.

Mara opened the door.

The hallway light was on — the same yellowish flicker — and standing in it was a woman. She was wearing clothes Mara didn't recognise, a grey jacket too large for her, dark trousers. She was thin. She looked like she hadn't slept in a long time. Her hair was shorter, rougher, and there was a scar above her left eyebrow that Mara didn't have. But her face. Her eyes. The way she stood, weight shifted slightly to the right because of the knee she'd hurt falling off the grain silo when she was fourteen.

Mara looked at her.

She looked at Mara.

They stood there in the flickering hallway light, and the world became very small and very quiet, reduced to this single impossible fact: that Mara was looking at herself, and herself was looking back, and the contract she'd signed — all nine hundred and forty-three pages of it, with its clauses about quantum uniqueness and the inviolable physics of single-state existence — was wrong.

Or it wasn't wrong. And something much worse was true.

The woman in the hallway opened her mouth. Her voice was Mara's voice, but damaged somehow, hoarse, like she'd been breathing different air for a long time.

"They told me I wouldn't come back," she said.

And then, in the same instant, without thought or decision, they both reached up with their right hand and touched the back of their neck. The same hand. The same gesture. The same fingers finding the same small disc at the same moment, the way you'd reach for a scar you'd carried your whole life. Mara felt the raised ridge of her plug beneath her fingertips and watched the woman in the hallway do exactly the same thing, a mirror with a half-second delay that wasn't a delay at all — it was perfect synchronisation, two bodies running the same instinct from the same memory, and it told her everything she needed to know. This woman was her. Not a glitch, not an error, not a malfunction. Her. And the original Mara, the one who had walked into the scanner in Houston with legs that had carried her since birth — that Mara was gone. Had been gone since the first transfer. What stood on either side of this doorway were two copies, two inheritors, and neither of them had any more claim to the name than the other.

Mara stood in the doorway of her apartment — her apartment, her life, her name — and felt the ground shift beneath everything she thought she understood.

Behind her, the mug sat on the counter where she'd left it that morning. The bed was unmade. The window showed the Houston skyline, glittering and indifferent, and beyond it the thin dark line of the Tether rising into a sky that had never felt further away.

Two women with the same face and the same memories and the same scar on the same knee stood on either side of a threshold, and neither of them knew which one was the ghost.
