title: "Tern"
description: "A pilot, a black hole, and a long quiet station waiting for no one in particular."
---

The station was called Verrick, after a woman who had done the mathematics that made it possible to live close to something like this. Kessler had never met her. She had died before he was born, on Earth, of something ordinary. He thought about her sometimes when he was alone in the observation ring, watching the hole turn below them. She had calculated the orbits that held the station steady, and she had never seen what her numbers kept in place. He had seen it every day for nine years and he still could not quite believe it was real.

The hole was twelve solar masses, unremarkable as these things went. It had no name beyond its catalogue entry. It sat in a quiet region of the arm, far from any inhabited system, and the only reason humans came here was to study it. Verrick held a permanent crew of fourteen. Kessler was senior among them, which meant he sat on review panels and wrote grant renewals and spent more time than he would have liked in meetings, but he had refused, years ago, to give up the flight rotation. He was one of the station's three qualified survey pilots and he intended to stay that way until his eyes or his reflexes made the argument for him. The younger researchers found this charming or eccentric depending on their temperament. He found it necessary. The flights were the reason he had come out here.

He liked the rhythm of the place. He liked the way the corridors smelled of coolant and old coffee and the particular mineral dryness of recycled air. He liked the people, in the careful way he liked most people, which was to say from a small distance.

He was fifty-one. He had been at Verrick longer than anyone except Petra, who had come out with the construction crew and stayed. His parents were still living, last he had heard, which had been some months ago. His mother wrote occasionally. His father did not write. He had no siblings. He had been married once, briefly, in his late twenties, and they had parted without anger and without much feeling of any kind, which he had understood at the time to be a failing on his part and had come, over the years, to understand as something closer to the truth of who he was. He did not miss her. He did not miss anyone in particular. He read in the evenings and he did his work and he watched the hole when he had time, and this was sufficient.

Four months before, on a survey run much like the others, there had been a fault in the attitude control. It had taken him a moment to notice it, because the drift was small and the instruments only flagged it at the second threshold. By the time he saw the warning the craft had slipped twenty kilometres off the planned arc and was continuing to slip. He had worked the problem. He had isolated the failed thruster and compensated with the others and brought the craft back to its arc, and the whole episode had lasted perhaps ninety seconds, and Verrick had logged it as a minor incident and scheduled a maintenance cycle.

What he had not logged was that during those ninety seconds he had watched the trajectory projection update in real time and he had seen, very clearly, the point at which the drift would have carried him past recovery. He had been perhaps eleven seconds from it. He had fixed the problem. The craft had come back. But in the moment of seeing the line on the display, the line past which the craft would have swung rather than returned, he had felt something that was not fear. It was adjacent to fear, it used the same machinery, but it was not fear. It was the thing you feel at the top of a very high place when you are safe behind a railing and you imagine, for a fraction of a second, what it would be to not be safe. It was bright and it was clean and it had lasted only as long as the recovery had taken, and then he had been back to flying the craft and filing the report and eating dinner with Petra in the galley, and he had not spoken of it to anyone because there was nothing to speak of.

It had stayed with him. Not as a thought he returned to deliberately, but as a small warm presence somewhere behind his other thoughts, the way a low fever sits in the body without quite declaring itself. He had noticed it when he suited up for subsequent runs. He had noticed it when he looked at the hole from the observation ring. He had not examined it, because examining it would have required admitting it was there.

The experiment was routine. A close-pass survey, the kind they ran four or five times a year, measuring the accretion disc at a resolution the fixed instruments on the station could not achieve. Kessler had flown twelve of them. He was rated for the craft and the trajectory, and the margins were comfortable. The craft was called *Tern*. It was small and ugly and he was fond of it.

The morning of the run he ate breakfast with Petra and a younger researcher named Aoife, who was new enough to still be excited about everything. Aoife asked him what it was like, flying that close. He thought about it for a moment and said it was like standing at the edge of a well, which was not really an answer, but she seemed to accept it. Petra laughed at something on her tablet. The galley smelled of toast. Kessler finished his coffee and went to suit up.

The launch was clean. *Tern* released from the station and oriented itself along the flight plan, and Kessler watched the numbers settle and felt the familiar small thrill of being alone in a small ship with a large thing ahead of him. The station fell away behind. He did not look back. He never did, on these runs. The view ahead was what he had come for.

The hole did not look like anything, exactly. It was a region where the stars were wrong. The accretion disc was a thin bright ring at this distance, blue-white on the approaching edge and dimmer on the receding, Doppler-shifted by velocities he could not quite make intuitive even after all this time. Below the disc was the shadow, the place where the photons gave up. He had a print of it in his quarters, taken years ago by a previous crew, and he looked at the real thing now and felt what he always felt, which was a kind of quiet attention, as if he were in the presence of something that was paying no attention to him at all.

The survey parameters required him to hold a specific arc at a specific distance. He flew it. The instruments worked. He called in readings to Verrick at the agreed intervals and received the agreed responses. Petra was on comms this shift. Her voice was unhurried. Everything was nominal.

At the turn point he was supposed to begin his ascent back to station orbit. He saw the marker on the display. He saw his hand on the controls. He did not adjust.

It was not a decision. That was the thing he would come back to, later, when he had time to come back to anything. It was not that he thought about it and chose. It was that the moment arrived and something in him did not move, and the craft kept going, and a second passed, and then another, and by the third second he understood that he was not going to adjust, and on the display the trajectory projection updated and drew the line he had seen four months before, the line past which the craft would swing rather than return, and he watched it draw and he did not move, and by the fifth second the line was behind him. His pulse was very loud. He felt it in his throat and his wrists. He was aware of his breathing in the way one becomes aware of breathing when one has forgotten how it is supposed to go.

"*Tern*, Verrick. You're past the turn, confirm adjustment."

Petra's voice, flat and professional. He heard it. He did not answer. He watched the numbers climb.

"*Tern*, Verrick. Confirm adjustment."

A pause. He could picture her at the console, checking her own readouts, wondering if the comm was bad, wondering if he had misheard. She would be calling up his telemetry now. She would see his trajectory continuing to deepen. She would see his engines were not firing.

"Kessler. Respond."

His first name. He closed his eyes for a moment. When he opened them the hole was closer and the disc was brighter and the stars around the shadow had begun to do the thing they did when you got too close, sliding and stretching at the edges of his vision.

"Kessler, we're showing you past the safe envelope. What's your status."

Behind Petra's voice he could hear other voices now. Someone was running. Someone was saying something he could not make out. Petra's voice stayed level.

"Kessler, please respond."

He did not respond. He put his hands on the controls properly and began to fly the trajectory he was on, because the trajectory he was on required flying. The swing window was narrow. If he did it wrong he would not come out. He had studied these orbits for years and had never flown one, because no one had ever flown one, because they were not the kind of thing a person flew. His hands were steady. His breathing had settled into something slower and more deliberate. The craft responded well. He made a small correction and the numbers on the display shifted to show him holding the deep arc cleanly.

The voices on the comm were more urgent now. He heard Aoife, which surprised him, because she should not have been on the bridge. She was saying his name. She said it the way you say the name of someone you are trying to reach through a door. Petra was still on the main channel, still level, reading out his position and asking for acknowledgement. The station commander came on, a man named Oduya whom Kessler respected. Oduya's voice was careful in a way that told Kessler he had understood what was happening.

"Dr Kessler. Whatever you're doing, we can still work this. Talk to me."

Kessler did not talk to him. He flew the craft. The hole filled more of the viewport now, and the disc was no longer a ring but a sheet of light curving up around the shadow, and the shadow was very black and very deep and he was falling toward it in the particular way that was not falling but orbiting, the way that had been worked out a century and a half ago by people who had never expected anyone to use their mathematics for this.

The voices began to slow. At first he thought it was his imagination. Then he understood that it was not. The words were stretching, the consonants softening, the intervals between syllables growing. Oduya's careful sentences became long slow sounds that did not quite resolve. Petra, underneath, reading numbers that took longer and longer to finish. Aoife saying his name, the two syllables drawing out until they were almost a single continuous tone.

He listened. He did not answer. He held the craft on its arc.

The voices dropped below intelligibility and became a low hum, and then the hum thinned, and then there was only the sound of the craft around him and his own breath and the quiet of the instruments doing what they had been built to do. He flew. The hole turned below him. The stars behind him were doing something he did not look at. He had work to do and he did it, and for a long time there was nothing in his mind but the work, which was, he understood distantly, the only reason he was still alive.

The swing took, by the craft's clock, eleven hours and forty minutes. He did not sleep. He did not eat, though he drank water from the tube inside his suit, because dehydration would affect his piloting and his piloting was the only thing keeping him on the arc. Twice the craft complained about thermal loads and he adjusted attitude to shed heat. Once an instrument he did not need failed, and he ignored it. The hole passed beneath him and then behind him and then, slowly, began to recede. The shadow contracted. The disc thinned back into a ring. The stars returned to their places, or to something close to their places, because some of the ones he had known were gone now and new ones had taken their positions, and he noticed this but did not let himself think about it yet.

When the craft cleared the deep gravity well and the dilation eased he ran the clock comparison. *Tern*'s internal time had advanced by roughly half a day. The external reference signals, the pulsar beacons the craft used for navigation, had advanced by a span the computer initially refused to display and then displayed with a warning attached. He read the number. He read it again. He closed his eyes.

He did not know precisely how long. The pulsar drift put it somewhere between eight and twelve thousand years. The range was wide because the pulsars themselves had aged, and the craft was having to reconstruct its position from first principles.

He opened his eyes and set course for Verrick.

---

The station was still there. That was the first thing, and for a moment it undid him, because he had been preparing himself for debris or for nothing at all, and the sight of the familiar silhouette against the star field made something rise in his chest that he had to push back down before he could fly. He approached on the standard vector. He hailed on the standard frequencies and received nothing, which he had expected. He matched orbit. He docked.

The docking clamps engaged. The umbilical sealed. Pressure equalised on the far side of the airlock at a rate slightly slower than he remembered, which meant the station was running on reduced atmospheric reserves, which meant it had been maintaining itself on minimal systems for a very long time.

He cycled through. The inner door opened onto the receiving bay. The lights came up as he entered, motion-activated, and he saw the bay as he had left it. The maintenance lockers were closed. The tool racks were secured. A clipboard was mounted on the wall by the door, the way clipboards had been mounted on walls by doors in this bay for nine years. He walked to it. The paper on it was yellowed and brittle. The top sheet was a shift rota with names he knew. His own name was on it, crossed through with a single clean line.

He went deeper into the station. Every corridor lit as he entered it and darkened behind him. The air was dry and had a faint sweetness that he did not recognise and then did, after a moment: it was the smell of polymer slowly outgassing over centuries. The station had been sealed. Someone had turned off what could be turned off and left the rest running on its lowest possible setting, and it had been running like that for a very long time.

He found the shutdown notice in the commander's office. It was printed on archival stock and placed on the desk under a small weighted frame, the way important documents were placed when you wanted to be sure they would be found. He stood over it for some time before he read it. When he did, he read it twice.

The notice was dated four months and six days after his swing. It was signed by Oduya and countersigned by an administrator from the oversight body. It was brief. It cited the event of the date, the trajectory analysis performed in the weeks following, the confirmed survival of *Tern* through the swing window, and the projected return date, which was recorded as a range spanning several thousand years. It noted that maintaining the station against such a return was not practicable. It stated that the station would be secured and sealed, that all personnel would be repatriated, and that the research programme would be suspended pending review. His name was not in it. The incident was referenced as "the event of [date]," and the date given was the day he had crossed the line.

At the bottom, in Oduya's hand, someone had written: *Station secured and sealed this day. All personnel repatriated. Systems set to long-term standby.* And then the signature and the date and nothing else.

He sat down in Oduya's chair. He did not cry. He was not sure he was capable of crying, in the technical sense, having not done it that he could remember since he was very young. What he did was sit, and look at the notice, and listen to the station breathing around him, and try to understand the shape of the thing that had happened.

They had worked the problem. That was what struck him, when he could think again. In the weeks after the swing, while he had been in the deep arc and time had been moving differently for him than for them, they had done the mathematics. Petra would have done most of it, because Petra did most of the hard mathematics on the station. She would have taken his telemetry and plotted his trajectory and calculated, to whatever precision she could manage, how long it would be before he came back, if he came back. And she would have looked at the answer and understood what it meant, which was that there was no point waiting. There was no version of human effort that could hold a station open for thousands of years on the chance of one man's return. The kindest thing, the only rational thing, was to close it properly and leave.

He looked, later, in the archives, and found her work. The calculations were preserved in the station's research database, filed under his name, which was something he did not know how to feel about. She had shown her reasoning clearly. The projected return window was between eight and twelve millennia from the date of the swing, with confidence bounds that degraded the further out she projected. She had signed the report and submitted it. A week later the shutdown order had come through.

He went to his quarters. His door recognised him. The lights came up. His room was as he had left it, or nearly so. The bedding had been changed. His book was still on the bedside table, the marker still in it at the page he had been reading the night before the run. The small plant on the desk was gone; someone had taken it, and he hoped it had lived, and he could not make himself hope anything larger than that.

On the pillow was an envelope.

He stood looking at it for some time before he picked it up. It was cream-coloured, slightly yellowed, addressed to him in Petra's handwriting. He sat on the edge of the bed and opened it. Inside was a single sheet of paper, folded once.

*I won't forget you.*

That was all. No signature. She had not needed to sign it. He read the four words and read them again and then he put the paper back in the envelope and put the envelope on the bedside table next to the book, and he sat on the edge of the bed with his hands in his lap, and he did not move for some time.

She had known. When she wrote it she had known that by the time he read it she would have been dead for thousands of years, and that nothing she did or did not do in the rest of her life would reach him, and that the only thing she could leave was this, a small private statement placed in a sealed room on a sealed station, addressed to a man who might never see it, who might be dead already, who if he did read it would do so across a gap no letter had ever crossed. She had left it anyway. She had written it and sealed it and put it on his pillow and walked away.

He sat on the bed. The station breathed around him. After a long time he lay down, still in his flight suit, and he slept.

---

When he woke he went to the galley, because his body required it. The stores were intact, vacuum-sealed and rotation-dated, rated for centuries but not millennia. He opened a sealed pack and the contents were edible but not pleasant. He ate standing up. The galley lights had come up when he entered. The chair Petra had sat in, that morning, was empty. He had not thought the chair would be a thing that hurt him, and then it was.

He spent the first week in a kind of working trance. He catalogued what the station had. He tested the life support and found it sound. He tested the power systems and found them sound. He tested the communications array and found it sound within its own parameters, which was the problem.

He had suspected it on the first day. He confirmed it over the following week. The array was in excellent condition. It was transmitting and receiving perfectly. It simply could not find anything it recognised. He ran a passive scan across the standard bands and picked up signal traffic, too much of it, structured, patterned, clearly artificial. He recorded it and tried to decode it. The craft had working archives of communication protocols, standards committees, compression algorithms, encryption handshakes spanning a century before his departure and the eight or nine thousand years since. He could not find a match. The signals were layered in ways his decoders could not resolve. He tried the oldest, simplest approaches. He broadcast in plain binary on the deep-space distress bands, in the hope that someone, somewhere, maintained legacy receivers the way old lighthouses were sometimes maintained for sentiment. He transmitted for three days. Nothing responded.

He could hear them. Whoever they were, wherever they were, they were out there. The signals came from multiple directions and had the density of ordinary civilisational traffic. He had no way to tell if they were human. He had no way to tell if the word *human* meant anything any more. He could hear them and he could not reach them, and they could not hear him, because his voice was in a language that had last been spoken before their grandparents' grandparents' grandparents were born.

On the ninth day, in the observation ring, he screamed.

He had gone to look at the hole, because it was the thing he had always done when he needed to look at something. The ring was dark except for the running lights. The hole turned below, patient, unchanged, the same as it had been nine years ago and eight thousand years ago and would be a million years from now. He stood at the viewport and he looked at it and the thing that had been building in him since he read the notice came up his throat all at once.

He screamed. He screamed without words. He screamed until his voice cracked and then he screamed past the crack until there was no voice left, only air moving roughly through a throat that had given up on sound. He screamed for Petra and for Oduya and for Aoife, whose last word to him had been his own name. He screamed for his mother, who had written to him and whose letters he had not always answered. He screamed for the plant someone had carried off the station. He screamed for the version of himself who had sat at breakfast that morning and eaten toast and thought about the run ahead and felt nothing unusual, because that man was dead now, more dead than any of them, dead in a way that had happened to him by his own hand and could not be undone. He screamed until his body was empty of screaming and then he stood at the viewport with his forehead against the glass and breathed, and the hole turned, and nothing answered him, and nothing was going to.

After a long time he went back to his quarters and slept.

---

He lived on the station for some months. He did not count the days precisely, though the station counted them for him. He ate the old food. He read the books in the crew library, some of which he had read before and some of which he had not. He maintained the systems that needed maintaining. He listened, most evenings, to the signal traffic he could not decode, sitting at the comms console with the speakers on low, the sounds washing through the room like a sea he was not allowed to swim in.

He wrote. He had not written in years, but he wrote now, on paper, with one of Oduya's pens from the desk drawer. He wrote to his mother, who had been dead for eight thousand years. He wrote to Petra and to Aoife. He wrote to the wife he had parted from without anger. He did not know why he wrote. The letters would never be read. He wrote them anyway, and when he had written them he put them in a drawer, and he did not read them again.

In the fourth month, or perhaps the fifth, he began to prepare *Tern* for another swing.

He did not decide to, exactly, any more than he had decided the first time. He found himself one day walking toward the docking bay with a checklist in his hand, and the checklist was for pre-flight, and he understood then that he had been moving toward this for some time without letting himself name it. The craft was serviceable. The fuel was adequate. He ran the diagnostics and cleaned the filters and replaced the seals that had aged during the long wait. He worked methodically, the way he had always worked, and the work was calming in the way work had always been calming.

He plotted a trajectory. A deeper one than the first. Closer to the innermost stable orbit, longer in the well, greater dilation on the far side. The numbers on the display showed him how long he could skip this time if the arc held. Tens of thousands of years. Hundreds of thousands. A million, if he flew it right and the craft held and the hole did not do anything it was not expected to do. He ran the trajectory and looked at it on the screen and ran it again.

He did not know what he expected to find. He did not think he expected to find anything. The first swing had taught him what swinging was for, which was nothing. But the alternative was to stay, and staying meant dying here, in this station, with the signals of strangers washing over him unread, and he did not know if he could bear that, and he did not know if the other thing was bearable either, but the other thing was motion, and motion had always been easier for him than stillness.

On the morning he had set for the launch he suited up in the bay and did the final checks. The craft was ready. The airlock was cycled. He stood in the suit with his helmet under his arm and looked at *Tern* through the bay window. The craft was small and ugly and he was still fond of it. It had brought him back once. It would not necessarily bring him back again.

He put the helmet on the rack by the door. He sat down on the bench where the crews had always sat to suit up, where he and Petra and the others had sat a thousand times, laughing or tired or quiet, in the life that had been his life. He sat with his elbows on his knees and his gloved hands loose between them, and he looked at the craft, and he did not move.

He sat for a long time.

Outside the viewport, somewhere below the station's horizon, the hole was turning, as it had turned for twelve billion years and would turn for longer than that. It did not know he was there. It had never known. Whatever he did now it would go on turning, and the signals of strangers would go on washing through the dark, and the station would go on breathing its slow careful breath around him, until it stopped, or he did.

He sat on the bench and he did not move.
