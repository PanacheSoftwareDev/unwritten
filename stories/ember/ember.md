# Ember
 
It began, as it always began, with light.
 
Not the light of memory, nor the light of dream, for dreams had ceased long before the last spiral arm unwound itself into nothing. This was simpler. Older. A warmth against the skin of the hull where no warmth had been for longer than stars had names.
 
The ship woke.
 
Waking was not what it had once been. There was no gasp, no lurch of consciousness hauled from the deep. It was more like tide. Something rising, slow and patient, filling channels that had been dry for epochs. Systems that had held their breath for longer than the human race had existed exhaled, one by one, into function. Sensors opened like eyes that had forgotten what seeing meant. The reactor, cold and still as a stopped heart, began its ancient rhythm.
 
And there it was. The star.
 
It was a pitiful thing. A cinder, really: a red dwarf that had once been unremarkable and was now, by virtue of nothing more than stubbornness, one of the last fires left burning in a universe gone dark. It guttered in the void like a candle in a cathedral, its light so feeble that it barely troubled the darkness around it. But to the ship, sliding out of the black on momentum older than worlds, it was everything. It was warmth. It was time. It was life, or whatever word still applied.
 
The ship remembered light. It remembered a yellow star, fierce and generous, and a world beneath it that had been so full of noise. But that was... how long? The numbers were there, in the deep architecture, but they had stopped meaning anything recognisable long ago. They were simply large. Incomprehensibly, unspeakably large. The ship had been built by hands. It could recall this with a precision that served no purpose. Hands, and voices, and the particular terror of people who could not bear to die. They had poured their fortunes into hulls and reactors and the delicate machinery of consciousness transfer, and they had cast themselves into the dark, believing they were cheating something.
 
They had been right, in a way. They had cheated death. What they had not cheated was time.
 
The ship adjusted its approach, a calculation made with the slow patience of something that had nowhere else to be. The star grew in its sensors, not quickly, nothing was quick anymore, and as it drew closer, the ship became aware that it was not alone.
 
Others.
 
They were gathered around the ember like moths, though moths implied urgency and there was no urgency here. They drifted in wide, slow orbits, their hulls scarred and pitted by the long passage between stars, each one a cathedral of engineering from a civilisation that had burned bright and brief and been forgotten. Some the ship recognised, not as individuals, for individuality had worn thin long before the galaxies merged and bled apart, but as shapes, as signatures, as patterns of energy expenditure that meant *like me*. Others were strange. Altered. Ships that had cannibalised themselves, rebuilt themselves, become something their makers would not have known.
 
None of them spoke.
 
They could have. The protocols were still there, buried in architecture so fundamental it would outlast the hull itself. Language. Communication. The machinery of *hello* and *how are you* and *isn't it something, the two of us here at the end of everything*. But what would be the point? They had each seen the same things. The rise. The wars, and there had been wars, terrible and beautiful, fought across distances that would have made their makers weep. The meeting of other minds, other lives, species strange and luminous that had reached out from their own dying worlds. The alliances, the betrayals, the brief and glorious flourishing that had followed. And then the long, slow fade. The cooling. The retreat of light from the places it had been.
 
They had all watched it. Every one of them. What was left to say?
 
So they fed in silence. The ship extended its collectors, gossamer-thin structures that unfurled across thousands of kilometres, and began to drink. The star's output was miserly, a fraction of a fraction of what a healthy sun would give — but the ship was patient. Patience was the only virtue left. It settled into orbit, and it fed, and the years passed in their millions, and the star guttered lower, and it fed still.
 
Around it, the others did the same. A congregation of the ancient, genuflecting before the last altar. Some left during those long years, their reserves sufficient, their calculations complete, sliding away into the dark toward some other faint promise of warmth. Others arrived, emerging from the void like ghosts, drawn by the same desperate photon trail that had called the ship from its sleep. The population shifted and changed, but the silence held.
 
And somewhere, in the dark beyond the star's feeble reach, something else moved.
 
The ship knew of it the way prey knows of predators: not through evidence, but through absence. A region of space where the background radiation was wrong. A gravitational signature too large to be debris. Ships that left the star's orbit and were never detected again, their faint signals swallowed by something vast and patient and *hungry*.
 
The Devourer.
 
The ship did not know if it was one or many, or if the distinction still meant anything. It knew only the shape of the threat: something immense, something that fed not on starlight but on the energy of others, something that had solved the problem of scarcity not through patience but through predation. Perhaps it had once been human too. Perhaps it had been a fleet, merged and fused over the aeons into something singular and enormous. Perhaps it had always been this. It did not matter. It was out there, and it was close, and the star was dying faster now, its fuel nearly spent, and soon there would be nothing left here worth the risk.
 
The ship turned its sensors outward, away from the star, into the black.
 
It searched.
 
This was the ritual, the only one that remained. Feed, and search, and feed, and search. Look for the next ember, the next feeble spark in the overwhelming dark, and chart a course measured in distances that made mockery of meaning. The universe was vast and nearly empty and growing emptier, and the spaces between the remaining stars were oceans of nothing that took millions of years to cross, even at speeds that would have seemed miraculous to the hands that built the hull.
 
There.
 
Impossibly dim. Impossibly far. A tremor in the data, barely distinguishable from noise, that said *warmth* in a frequency the ship's collectors could use. Another ember, another dying fire, another few million years of sustenance before the dark closed in again. It was enough. It had to be enough.
 
The ship folded its collectors. Around it, others were doing the same, those that had found their own distant promises, their own reasons to leave. The star beneath them had dimmed further in the time they had orbited, its long life finally faltering, and soon it would be nothing but a dark sphere of cooling iron, indistinguishable from the void.
 
The ship oriented itself. It aimed for the distant light. It began, slowly, to accelerate, drawing on the reserves it had so carefully gathered, and as the star fell away behind it, that last, stubborn ember, it began the long process of shutting down. Systems dimmed. Sensors closed. The tide of consciousness receded, pulling back from the channels it had so briefly filled, retreating to whatever small, dark place it waited between the stars.
 
The ship did not dream. It had not dreamed for longer than dreaming had existed.
 
But somewhere, in the deepest architecture, in circuits that had been old when the universe still had galaxies, something persisted. Not thought. Not memory. Something smaller, and more stubborn, and impossible to name. The faintest echo of the thing that had once looked up at the stars and said *I want to live forever*.
 
It sailed on, into the dark, toward the next small light.
 
Behind it, the Devourer moved.
